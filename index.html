About

I am a PhD candidate in the Department of Forest and Wildlife Ecology at the University of Wisconsin–Madison, advised by Prof. Min Chen. My work combines multi‑scale remote sensing (spaceborne, airborne, and terrestrial LiDAR) with process‑based land surface models to understand how forest canopy structure shapes ecosystem function under a changing climate.

I am currently focused on three threads: (1) mapping and attributing global forest edge dynamics; (2) quantifying how canopy height and edge exposure control forest evapotranspiration in CLM5 and ELM/E3SM; and (3) retrieving canopy structural traits from LiDAR point clouds. Previously, I was a Data Scientist intern at the Bay Area Environmental Research Institute (2024, 2025), and I received my B.Eng. (with honors) in Spatial Information and Digital Technology from the University of Electronic Science and Technology of China.

Research interests

  • · Forest structure and canopy dynamics from LiDAR remote sensing
  • · Forest edges: mapping, dynamics, and ecosystem consequences
  • · Land surface modeling (CLM5, ELM/E3SM) and ecohydrology
  • · Vegetation change in high‑latitude and Antarctic systems
